Core Reading: Different Ways to Stay and Play

When planning a vacation, the most crucial decision is often where you will stay. This choice can lead to entirely different experiences, as seen in the lives of Falah from Jordan and Andro from Croatia, or in the busy daily operations of the Hillbrow Hotel in Dublin.

Falah Rhiladi lives in the bustling city of Amman, but his roots are in the desert. His grandfather used to follow the traditional nomadic life, moving his home across the sands. While Falah is a self-proclaimed "city boy" who would never give up his urban comforts, he still feels the pull of the wilderness. Once a year, he goes camping with his father. He loves the peace of the desert, claiming that you wouldn't believe how many stars you can see once you leave the city's pollution behind. If he didn't have these annual trips, he would probably feel much more stressed by city life.

Similarly, Andro Zelic grew up with a deep love for the outdoors. As a child, his family used to go to the same campsite every year on the Croatian coast. He fondly remembers a "gang" of children who always used to see each other and run around until the early hours. "We never used to go to bed before one in the morning," he recalls. Even though Andro can afford luxury hotels today, he still prefers camping. He believes that if he stayed in a hotel, his children wouldn't have the same freedom to explore. To him, hotels often have a cold atmosphere compared to the friendly vibe of a campsite.

However, hotels remain the primary choice for many travelers. David recently called the Hillbrow Hotel to inquire about a room. He learned that while the hotel is convenient, it comes with extra costs. For instance, if guests wanted Wi-Fi, they would have to pay a fee. Furthermore, if they decided to cancel late, they would lose their 10% deposit.

The life of a hotel receptionist is never dull, especially when dealing with guests in the presidential suite. Lady Zaza has a severe nut allergy; if she accidentally ate a peanut, it would be a medical emergency. If the hotel didn't remove all green items from her room, she would likely refuse to stay there. She also requested hundreds of flowers because arranging them is the only thing that relaxes her.

Whether it is the simplicity of a desert tent, the nostalgia of a coastal campsite, or the high-maintenance luxury of a suite, how we travel reflects who we are. As Andro suggests, if more people chose camping, they would perhaps find a warmer way of life. But for those like Lady Zaza, the comfort of a hotel is a necessity she would never trade for a night under the stars.

1-3. 📖 Reading: Camping Experiences

Falah Rhiladi, Jordan

I live and was brought up in Amman, the capital of Jordan, but I'm actually from a family of Bedouin. My grandfather used to follow the traditional nomadic life, moving from place to place. I'm a real city boy now and I would never give it up. However, I usually go camping in the desert once a year with my father. I really love it. You feel a peace you can never get in the city with its noise and pollution. You feel closer to nature and God in the desert, and you wouldn't believe how many stars you can see!

Andro Zelic, Croatia

We used to go to the same campsite every year when I was a kid. It's a place on the coast not far from Split. It was a different world to Zagreb. We always used to see the same people there. There was a gang of us that just ran around all day. We went to the beach, played table tennis or football, explored the campsite and played hide and seek: we just did whatever we liked and we never used to go to bed before one in the morning. I can afford to stay in hotels now with my family, but to be honest, we prefer to camp. Hotels have a cold atmosphere. They're less friendly and there's not the same freedom and, actually, there are often more facilities at campsites - especially for kids.

Section 3: Writing Practice

Step 1. Grammar & Vocabulary Check:

Grammar Mastery

1. Past Habits & States (used to)

• used to + verb: Used to talk about past habits or states that are no longer true now.

• never used to + verb: Used for things that did not happen regularly in the past.

  • • Example: "My grandfather used to follow the traditional nomadic life." (Core Reading - Falah's family history)
  • • Example: "We never used to go to bed before one in the morning." (Core Reading - Andro's childhood camping)

2. Imaginary Situations (Second Conditional)

• If + Past Simple, ...would + verb: Used to talk about imaginary or unlikely situations in the present or future.

• wouldn't: Used to express a negative result in an imaginary situation.

  • • Example: "If she accidentally ate a peanut, it would be a medical emergency." (Core Reading - Lady Zaza's allergy)
  • • Example: "You wouldn't believe how many stars you can see!" (Core Reading - Falah's camping experience)

3. Conditional Intentions & Will

• would / wouldn't (Volition): Used to show willingness or refusal to do something in a specific context.

  • • Example: "I am a real city boy and I would never give it up." (Core Reading - Falah's lifestyle choice)
  • • Example: "She would likely refuse to stay there." (Core Reading - Lady Zaza's reaction)

Context from our Reading:

  • "If guests wanted Wi-Fi, they would have to pay a fee."
  • "We used to go to the same campsite every year."
  • "If more people chose camping, they would perhaps find a warmer way of life."
