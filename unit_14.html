Core Reading: Things, Choices, and Our Planet

Every day, we are surrounded by things. Some are the objects that we buy to make our lives easier, while others are the presents that we receive from people we love. However, as Professor Liam Taylor points out, every object has a story and an environmental cost. Whether it is the machine that makes hot water in your kitchen or the mountain bike that you ride to work, we must think about what we own and how we use it.

When moving into a new place, we often realize how many basic items we lack. In a conversation between three friends, they discuss the things that they need for cleaning and daily life. They talk about a dustpan, which is the thing that you use to get rubbish from the floor with a brush, and bleach, which is a kind of strong chemical liquid that is good for cleaning the floor.

They also need to care for their clothes. They look for an iron, which is the machine that you use for the clothes after you wash them, and an ironing board, which is the thing that you put the clothes on when you use the iron. In the bathroom, they need a shower curtain, which is the plastic thing that stops the water from leaving the shower, and a shower rail to hold it. Finally, they cannot live without a kettle, which is the machine that makes hot water for tea.

The objects we own often come to us as gifts. These gifts can be digital, practical, or even strange. One person received his own website, which was something fun that he and his sister could work on together. Another person got a mountain bike, which has become one of the most useful presents ever because it saves him loads of money on petrol. However, not all gifts are perfect. One woman describes an iron as a "bad present" from an ex-boyfriend because of the message it sent about their relationship. On the other hand, a simple rock from a beach can be a great gift. Although it seemed like a thing that was a bit stupid at first, it became a precious memory because it was something that he would never forget.

While we enjoy these objects, we must consider their impact on the planet. Professor Taylor explains that there are laws of nature that we cannot ignore, such as gravity and waste. Everything we consume leads to waste. For example, kiwi fruits that are covered in clear plastic and flown thousands of miles create a huge amount of carbon dioxide.

The materials used in our products are also important. We must remember that the aluminium in soda cans is bad because companies must mine four tonnes of rock just to get one tonne of aluminium. The transport costs are higher because glass is heavier. If you must have soft drinks, you should buy them in recyclable plastic bottles that are the largest size, as they are more efficient.

We often believe that biodegradable items disappear, but archaeologists recently investigated sites and found newspapers that were thirty years old and perfectly preserved sausages. Therefore, we mustn't be careless with our choices. We must accept our responsibility to buy local products and avoid unnecessary packaging. By understanding the things that we use every day, we can make better decisions for our future.

1-3. 📖 Reading: RUBBISH FOOD

There are laws of nature that we can't ignore, like gravity and waste. We know what goes up must come down and, similarly, we can't avoid the fact that everything we produce and consume leads to waste. In the case of my supermarket shopping, there's loads of waste. Professor Liam Taylor, an expert on the environment, is trying to convince me I could waste less.

From my shopping basket, he picks up a polystyrene tray of six New Zealand kiwi fruits covered in clear plastic. "These probably caused three tons of carbon dioxide by being flown twelve thousand miles. To make things worse, this kind of plastic is almost impossible to recycle. What's wrong with local apples?"

'Nothing', I weakly reply, 'I just prefer kiwis.'

'Hmm. Well, if you must have them, eat Italian ones – and buy them with no packaging.'

He looks at the bottles of water and cans of cola. Before he can say anything, I say, 'I'm always careful to recycle those.'

'Well, that's good, but the aluminium in those cans is bad. They must mine four tonnes of rock to get one tonne of aluminium, and the transport costs of the cans and glass bottles are higher as they are heavier than plastic. Anyway, what's wrong with having tap water? It's much more efficient.'

'I... er ... prefer ... .' The professor's look stops me from finishing the sentence!

'If you must have soft drinks, buy them in recyclable plastic bottles and get the largest size, because they use less plastic than lots of small bottles. The same is true of those small boxes of cereal.'

The next problem is the amount of meat I've bought. He tells me the chicken is OK, but generally meat is bad for the environment. Firstly, cows and sheep produce a lot of natural gas which causes global warming. Secondly, they're an inefficient way to get food energy. Better to be vegetarian, especially if the vegetables are locally grown and you don't eat too many dairy products.'

I am becoming depressed as all my favourite things get crossed off my shopping list. 'What about those cakes?' I say. 'They were made in the supermarket bakery and the packaging is biodegradable.'

He laughs. 'Well, I guess the cake is OK, but forget about biodegradable! Apparently, a team of archaeologists recently investigated sites where rubbish had been buried. They found newspapers that were thirty years old, and which you could still read, next to perfectly preserved sausages!'

I feel slightly sick and very, very guilty.

Step 1. Grammar & Vocabulary Check:

Grammar Mastery

1. Relative Clauses (Describing Things)

• that / which: Used to give more information about an object or to define what something is.

• describing purpose: Often used with "the thing that..." or "the machine that..." to explain what an object does.

  • • Example: "It's the machine that makes hot water for tea." (Section 1 - Describing a kettle)
  • • Example: "I bought a dress that he really liked." (Section 2 - Present from an ex-boyfriend)

2. Strong Obligation & Necessity

• must: Used to express a strong necessity or a rule that we cannot ignore.

• mustn't: Used to say that something is prohibited or a very bad idea.

  • • Example: "We must think about what we own." (Core Reading - Environmental responsibility)
  • • Example: "We mustn't be careless with our choices." (Reading - Advice on waste)

3. Conditional Advice

• If + must...: Used to give specific advice or alternatives when someone insists on doing something.

  • • Example: "If you must have them, eat Italian ones." (Reading - Choosing local fruit)
  • • Example: "If you must have soft drinks, buy large bottles." (Reading - Tips for recycling)

Context from our Reading:

  • "There are laws of nature that we can't ignore."
  • "They must mine four tonnes of rock for aluminium."
  • "It is something that he would never forget."
