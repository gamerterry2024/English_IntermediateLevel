Core Reading: The Full Story of Getting There

Planning a trip is often the most exciting part of a holiday, but as the travellers in today’s news found out, the actual journey can involve a lot of stress and complete chaos. Whether you are taking a train, catching a bus, or driving a car, there is always some risk of facing the unexpected. Today’s BBD travel report is a perfect example of how much trouble a single day can bring to the public.

First, let’s look at the airports. In France, an ongoing strike by the air traffic controllers is causing chaos. There is little hope of a deal being reached soon, so much of the French airspace remains closed. Consequently, some flights are delayed while a few others are cancelled. Passengers are advised to consult the airline's website for information before leaving the house. On the roads, a lorry carrying a huge load of watermelons crashed, leaving a big mess for the police to clear up. Furthermore, the M6 motorway is closed for repairs, and although there is a diversion in operation, the traffic is moving very slowly. Even the London Underground is struggling; the Northern Line has repair work, and East Ham station is closed because of a flood caused by a burst water pipe.

For one particular couple trying to reach the museum, the journey was the biggest challenge of the day. First, they realized they were lost and asked a stranger for the way. The man gave them some advice: "Take the second road on the right, cross the street, and find the stop opposite the town hall." However, because they didn't pay attention, they couldn't remember if it was the second right or the second left! They had no idea which bus to catch.

Later, they found a bus stop, but an old lady they spoke to couldn't hear the question. A kind person nearby explained that they were at the wrong stop because the bus went the wrong way. They had to go over the crossing, past the traffic lights, to find the number 67 bus. Even after finally getting on a bus, they got off at the wrong stop again! A final guide told them to walk past a church, a monument, and a football ground for about half a mile. "You can't miss it," he said. By the time they arrived, they had a lot of frustration between them.

Ultimately, the experience of getting there depends on the perspective you choose. As the story of the farmer and the two travellers teaches us, the kind of people and the kind of luck you find in a new town usually matches the attitude you bring with you. If you have too much negativity, you will only see the traffic and the strikes. But if you have patience and a sense of humour, you can survive any journey, even one filled with watermelons and floods.

1-3. 📖 Reading: The Two Travellers and the Farmer

One hot afternoon, a traveller was walking along a dusty country road when he suddenly noticed an old farmer working in the fields. Keen to rest his feet, the traveller stopped and called out to the farmer, who seemed happy enough to stop working and chat for a while. The farmer put down his tools, stretched his back and walked slowly over to the side of the road, where the two men sat down together and shared a glass of water.

After some brief introductions, the stranger asked, "What kind of people live in the next town?"

"What were the people like where you've come from?" replied the farmer, answering one question with another.

"They were a bad bunch. They were all troublemakers – and very lazy too! They were the most selfish people in the world. I didn't trust any of them and I have to say, I'm glad I'm leaving them all behind."

"Is that right?" replied the old farmer. "Well, I'm afraid I've got some bad news for you. In the town you come to, you'll find more of the same sort of people."

Disappointed, the traveller continued on his way, while the farmer returned to his work.

A couple of weeks later, another stranger appeared, coming from the same direction. He also slowed down as he walked through the fields and shouted out to the farmer. The two men stopped to talk and after a few minutes, the second traveller said, "Do you mind if I ask what kind of people live in the next town?"

"What were the people like where you've come from?" replied the farmer once again.

"They were the best people in the world. Hard-working, honest and friendly. I'm sorry I'm leaving them all behind."

"Is that right?" replied the old farmer. "Well, I'm happy to tell you that in the town you come to, you'll find more of the same sort of people."

Step 1. Grammar & Vocabulary Check:

Grammar Mastery

1. Articles (a, an and the)

• a / an: Used for singular, countable nouns when mentioned for the first time or not specific.

• the: Used for specific things, something already mentioned, or unique things (like "the sun").

  • • Example: "A lorry crashed on the road, leaving a big mess." (Section 0 - Traffic accident news)
  • • Example: "Take the second road and find the stop opposite the town hall." (Section 1 - Specific directions)

2. Much & Too Much (Uncountable Nouns)

• much: Used in negative sentences or questions to talk about a large amount of something you can't count.

• too much: Used to say there is more than enough (often a negative meaning).

  • • Example: "There isn't much space for cars to pass." (Section 0 - Road condition)
  • • Example: "I feel too much frustration with this journey!" (Section 2 - Alex's complaint)

3. Some & A bit of (Uncountable Nouns)

• some: Used in positive sentences to talk about an unspecified amount.

• a bit of / a little: Used for a small amount of something uncountable.

  • • Example: "Passengers can find some information on the website." (Section 1 - Advice for flight delays)
  • • Example: "The man gave them a bit of advice about the way." (Section 0 - Asking for directions)
