Core Reading: Communication, Services, and Critical Moments

In our modern world, we are more connected than ever. Whether it is a quick mobile phone call to a friend or an urgent 999 emergency dial, communication is the thread that holds our society together. However, this ease of connection comes with its own set of challenges. Sometimes, the lines of communication get crossed, leading to frustration, while at other times, a single message or photo can literally save a life.

In northern England, the police face a growing crisis. Every day, they have to answer three hundred 999 calls, but still, a third of them are found to be totally unnecessary. People have called the emergency line for trivial reasons, such as complaining about a broken TV. To combat improper calls, the police have already introduced £80 fines and are distributing leaflets to educate the public. Although they have started this campaign, many people haven't changed their habits yet.

While some use phones for minor issues, others have used them to perform miracles. In a remote town in Australia, a young boy suffered a serious head injury. His mother noticed a swelling on his head and rushed him to the hospital. The doctor, despite having never performed such an operation, had the presence of mind to call a colleague for help. He used a household drill to save the boy's life.

Similarly, a man bitten by a poisonous spider used his mobile phone to take a picture. This photo was sent to experts at a university, who identified the species and found a cure. Without that digital image, the doctor might not have known how to treat the poison in time.

However, technology and services are not always reliable. Take the case of Jaslyn, who tried to track two packages from Singapore. She was told they were being held due to insufficient postage. Despite being promised that they would arrive, she later discovered that one had already been returned. Similarly, many customers face awful service with their internet connections, where automated systems often drive them mad.

Whether we are dealing with a life-threatening situation or an annoying bill, how we communicate defines our daily experience. We have already seen how technology can save us, but we still have a long way to go in making sure our service systems function as they should.

1-3. 📖 Reading: News and Stories

1. Unnecessary 999 Calls in Northern England

Every day, the police in northern England have to answer three hundred 999 calls, but a third of them are totally unnecessary. Last year, the calls that police received included someone wanting a ride home, a man complaining about his TV not working, and two people who reported lost cats. A number of calls were also from young children who had accidentally dialled 999.

The police are still dealing with this problem, so they are sending leaflets to houses to explain the situation. They have already introduced £80 fines for those making improper calls to prevent further time-wasting.

2. A Miracle Operation in Remote Australia

Nicolas Rossi, a 13-year-old boy from a remote town in Australia, fell off his bicycle and hit his head. Although Nicolas initially seemed fine, his mother, a nurse, noticed a swelling on his head. She rushed him to the nearest hospital, where the doctor realized the boy was in danger of dying if he didn't have an operation to reduce the pressure on his brain.

Unfortunately, Doctor Carson had never done the operation before and only had a normal household drill. After cleaning the drill, he just phoned a colleague in Melbourne, who explained where and how to make the hole. The one-minute operation was successful, and the boy was transferred by plane to a Melbourne hospital. Nicolas has already made a complete recovery, and Carson described his actions as 'just part of the job'.

3. The Spider Photo that Saved a Life

Matthew Stevens, 23, was bitten by a Brazilian wandering spider in a pub in south-west England while he was cleaning the kitchen. The spider probably came into Britain in a box of bananas. After the incident, Mr. Stevens took a photo of the 12-centimetre spider with his mobile phone, but then went home, even though his hand was still swelling 'like a balloon'.

At home, he collapsed with breathing difficulties and had to be rushed to the hospital. Fortunately, he had already taken the picture, which he was able to show to the doctors. The photo was just sent to a university, where they identified the species and found a cure for the poison. He hasn't fully recovered yet, but he is out of danger thanks to that photo.

Section 3: Writing Practice

Step 1. Grammar & Vocabulary Check:

Grammar Mastery

1. Completed Actions (Already & Just)

• already: Used to say that something happened sooner than expected.

• just: Used to say that something happened a very short time ago.

  • • Example: "The police have already introduced £80 fines." (Section 1 - Action taken against improper calls)
  • • Example: "Doctor Carson just phoned a colleague for help." (Section 2 - Immediate action during the operation)

2. Expected & Continuing Actions (Yet & Still)

• yet: Used in negative sentences or questions to talk about something expected to happen.

• still: Used to say that a situation is continuing, especially when it should have finished.

  • • Example: "Many people haven't changed their habits yet." (Section 1 - Expected change in behavior)
  • • Example: "He is still in bed at 12 o'clock!" (Conversation 1 - A continuing state of sleeping)

3. Negative Contexts with Still & Yet

• still (negative): Often used to show surprise or annoyance that something hasn't happened.

• haven't + yet: A standard way to describe an uncompleted task.

  • • Example: "The wireless box still doesn't work properly." (Section 3 - Frustration with technology)
  • • Example: "He hasn't been able to talk to a person yet." (Core Reading - Jim's service complaint)

Context from our Reading:

  • "Nicolas has already made a complete recovery."
  • "The photo was just sent to a university."
  • "He is still waiting for a resolution."
