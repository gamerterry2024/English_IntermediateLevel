Core Reading: The Hidden Value of Wealth: From Daily Choices to a Secret Legacy

The story of wealth is often less about how much someone earns and more about the perspective they choose to have on life. When Laima and Aidan discussed their economic situations, it became clear that the climate of the market and unemployment opportunities dictate many personal decisions. Aidan had been living in a beautiful coastal area for almost two years. However, because the economy had been doing badly and unemployment had gone up over the last few months, he decided to move back to Canada. He had realized that salaries were higher back home, even though inflation had made everything more expensive. He knew that if he stays in his current location, he might not have a job in a year’s time.

While some move for better opportunities, others accumulate wealth through persistent frugality. A lawyer recently revealed a staggering secret about a man who had died two months earlier. For sixteen years, his children had assumed they were impoverished because they had worn second-hand clothes and had never been bought toys. Their father had been extraordinarily shrewd at investing. In 1965, he had bought shares that had grown into a fortune of £2.7 million by the time he passed away.

The children were astonished to learn that their father had lived so simply despite his inheritances. He had decided to leave most of his money to a charity for teenagers. This demonstrates that the love they had shared as a family was more important than the money they had lacked in their childhood.

In daily life, financial transactions often lead to more mundane complications. At a dinner, a man had intended to treat his friend, but he suddenly realized he had left his wallet at home. He promised, "I'll pay you back as soon as I can." In another situation, a customer was bargaining for a vintage jacket, but the seller refused to lower the price further, saying he had already lost money on the deal. Meanwhile, a student was worried because bus fares had just gone up, making it harder to save for a car.

Finally, we must exercise caution in the digital age. One woman was told by her bank that they suspected her card had been copied. Someone had used it for irregular activity overseas. After she receives her new card, she will be able to use her account again safely. Whether it is a small purchase or a multi-million pound legacy, these stories show that money had always been a complex part of the human experience.

By looking at these examples, we see how past actions—whether saving money or investing wisely—shape our future. If we manage our finances with a long-term perspective, we will be better prepared for whatever happens after a while.

None of us had any idea what was going to happen when we arrived at the lawyer's office. Dad had died two months earlier in his bed in the same two-bedroom house where we'd grown up. Apart from the house, we didn't expect Dad to leave anything of value. I mean, for years after my mum died, he'd gone to a neighbour's house to watch TV. I offered to buy him a TV once, but he just said, 'Never buy what you can borrow!' That was typical of him. I guess he liked his neighbour's company as well.

My mum had never worked and Dad was an insurance salesman. We assumed he wasn't successful because we were never bought toys and we wore second-hand clothes. We just thought he couldn't afford these things. Dad used to find bits of wood and turn them into toy boats and dolls. Mum taught us to make and repair clothes, which we used to do together at night. They had funny little sayings that they'd repeat whenever we complained about things: 'Money's silver, but a needle and thread is gold!'; 'Early to bed, early to rise, makes a man healthy, wealthy and wise'; 'Never buy what you can borrow, never throw away what you can repair.' We used to laugh at them, and sometimes invented our own silly sayings: 'A fool spends what the wise man saves'; 'A glass of water is worth all the tea in China.' We laughed, but having so little money was often annoying. I think we were the only family in our school without a TV; we never drank soft drinks, and sharing a rom with two big sisters for sixteen years was difficult.

So we walked into the lawyer's office and sat down. We were serious, but not sad any more. Dad had had a good life. The lawyer started reading; I was hardly paying attention, really, but then the numbers seemed to continue without end. 'Wait, I'm sorry,' I said. 'How much did you say he had?' The lawyer smiled, 'Yes, I imagine it does need repeating. Two million, seven hundred and eighty-one thousand, six hundred and fifty three pounds and eighteen pence.'

We didn't know what to say! Nearly three million pounds! How? Why? We had so many questions, so many feelings.

Step 1. Grammar & Vocabulary Check:

Grammar Mastery

1. The Past Perfect (Background Events)

• had + past participle: Used to talk about an action that happened before another action in the past.

• Negative (hadn't): Used to say that something had not happened by a certain time in the past.

  • • Example: "Dad had died two months earlier before we went to the office." (Reading - The sequence of events)
  • • Example: "I had assumed he was poor because of his old clothes." (Reading - A past belief before the discovery)

2. Future Time Clauses (Plans & Conditions)

• when / after / as soon as + Present Simple: Used to talk about future events. Even though we mean the future, we use the present tense after these words.

• Main Clause (will + verb): Used to describe the result or the next action.

  • • Example: "As soon as I get home, I will talk to my sisters." (Dialogue - Future plan after the meeting)
  • • Example: "You will receive a new PIN after you receive the card." (Listening - Bank security instruction)

3. Conditionals with If (Possibility)

• If + Present Simple: Used to describe a possible future condition.

• Result (will / might): Used to show what the outcome will be.

  • • Example: "If I don't buy it, someone else will." (Listening - Buying a car)
  • • Example: "If unemployment stays high, he will move to Canada." (Listening - Economic decision)
