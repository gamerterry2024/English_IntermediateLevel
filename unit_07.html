Core Reading: Not Just All in the Mind

We all face health problems from time to time. Some are physical, like a cold or the flu, while others are related to our mental health. Understanding how to deal with these situations is essential for a healthy life.

If you feel weak and tired, or if your muscles ache, you might be getting the flu. In this case, you should go home and get some rest. Don't stay at work or school because you might spread the virus to others. For those who suffer from hay fever during spring, you ought to wear sunglasses to protect your eyes. If you can't stop sneezing and your eyes water, why don't you visit the chemist's? They can recommend effective tablets to help you feel better.

Sometimes, we experience smaller but annoying problems like hiccups or indigestion. If you have gas or a bad stomach after a meal, take some indigestion tablets with water. For hiccups, some people suggest a strange cure: put your fingers in your ears and swallow water slowly. It might work for you!

In more serious cases, like an accident, you may need first aid. If you have a deep cut, a doctor might need to give you stitches. For a burn, apply some cream and dress the burn carefully to prevent infection. Consult your doctor if the pain continues.

However, we must remember that health is not just all in the mind, nor is it only about the body. Many people suffer from mild depression or anxiety, but they are afraid to ask for help because of negative attitudes in society. We often tell people to "cheer up," but this is not always helpful. Science shows that many mental illnesses have a physical or chemical cause in the brain. Therefore, we should treat mental health just like a physical illness. Recognise that it is a natural part of life and seek professional therapy or support when needed.

Why don't we start changing our attitudes today? Be kind to yourself and others, and take it easy when you are not feeling well.

1-3. 📖 NOT JUST ALL IN THE MIND

The problem is that when we think of mental health problems, we often think of the most serious cases: people hearing voices; people seeing things that aren't really there; mad people murdering other people. The reality is that very few mentally ill people are dangerous and a lot of mental problems are quite mild. They are similar to flu: you feel bad for a week or two and then you recover.

However, in the same way that you may need medical attention to overcome flu, so doctors can help cure mild depression or someone's anxiety about eating, for example. In fact, people control or completely recover from many of the more serious mental health problems through drugs, therapy or other support. For example, the mathematician John Nash, whose experiences were shown in the film A Beautiful Mind, overcame paranoid schizophrenia and went on to win a Nobel Prize.

Unfortunately, because of negative attitudes towards mental illnesses, many people don't ask for help and when they do, they often get an unwanted reaction such as 'Don't be silly!' or 'Cheer up! Don't be so sad.' For men, the problem may be even worse, because society expects them to be less emotional. For example, people say 'boys don't cry' and 'he's a strong, silent type.' Showing your emotions means you are seen as weak.

Dealing with mental health problems is always going to be difficult because it's hard to know what's happening in someone's head, but science is making progress. In fact, it's becoming clear that many mental illnesses have a physical/chemical cause in the brain and that early treatment is important. What we need to do now is to change our attitudes. We can begin by recognising that mental illness is a more natural part of life than we think.
