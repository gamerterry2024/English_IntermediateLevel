Core Reading: The Reality of Leaving Home: New Beginnings and Hard Lessons

Leaving home is an incredibly exciting moment in any young person’s life. Whether you are moving to a new country like Wales to do a Master’s, or just moving into a small flat in your hometown, independence brings a great sense of freedom. You can come and go whenever you want and eat whatever you like. However, as many students soon realize, living away from your parents is often much harder than it first appears.

One of the most depressing things you learn is that everything costs money. When you live at home, you might not notice how much is spent on electricity, water, or even a pint of milk. Many people move out with a healthy bank balance, but after buying some furniture and new clothes, they suddenly find their cash machine card is rejected. While banks are always happy to lend you money, it is essential to learn how to manage a budget so you do not have to borrow. As the saying goes, "money doesn't grow on trees."

Another challenge is the physical work of running a house. At home, life is easy because you don't always have to do the washing, iron your clothes, or fix things when they break. Once you are on your own, knowing how to use basic tools or setting a washing machine to the correct temperature makes a huge difference to your quality of life. You might not miss your parents at first, but you will definitely miss their cooking. If you don't start learning now, you will end up living on frozen meals and takeaway pizzas, which is bad for both your health and your pocket.

Sharing a space can also be complicated. Flat-sharing might sound like a thoughtful way to live with friends, but it is often the easiest way to lose them. You will argue about whose turn it is to clean the toilet or who used the last of the bread. Whether you are staying with a host family who has strict house rules—like no overnight guests or being vegetarian—or living in a noisy block with five other students, you must learn to be quiet and respectful.

Finally, you have to face the fact that living alone can be lonely. Even in an exciting city like Muscat or a lovely coastal town, you might feel homesick. It is tiring to speak a foreign language all day or deal with a different culture. You might try to fill your time with a busy social life, but sooner or later, you must learn to enjoy your own company. If you can master these challenges, you might just become the best friend you’ll ever have.

1-3. 📖 Reading: FIVE THINGS YOU SHOULD KNOW BEFORE LEAVING HOME

1. MONEY DOESN'T GROW ON TREES

One of the most depressing things you learn after leaving home is that everything costs money. You move out with a healthy bank balance, you buy a few bits of furniture and some new jeans and before you know it, the cash machine has eaten your card! Of course, banks will lend you as much money as you want, but remember — you don't have to borrow! Learning to manage a budget may be boring, but it's essential.

2. YOU CANNOT LIVE ON BREAD ALONE

Although you may not miss your parents very much when you first leave home, you can bet you'll miss their cooking! Learning to cook well takes time, so start learning now. If you don't, you'll end up living on frozen meals and takeaway pizzas, and that'll add kilos to your weight and take pounds from your pocket!

3. MACHINES ARE YOUR FRIENDS

While you are still living at home, life is easy. You don't have to do the washing, iron your clothes, cook, shop, or fix things. Once you're on your own, though, knowing how to do simple things like setting a washing machine to the correct temperature or using basic tools can make a huge difference to your quality of life!

4. FLAT-SHARING IS THE EASIEST WAY TO LOSE FRIENDS

Living with your best friends might sound like a really great idea, but trust me — it isn't! You'll argue about everything and nothing. You'll shout at each other about whose turn it is to clean the toilet and about who paid for the last pint of milk and before you know it, you'll be enemies rather than friends!

5. LIVING ON YOUR OWN CAN BE LONELY

Living away from home can be incredibly exciting. You can come and go whenever you want; you can eat whatever you want and wear what you want, but spending time on your own can be hard. You may want to fill your time with a social life, meeting people and going to parties, but sooner or later you have to learn how to enjoy your own company. You might just become the best friend you'll ever have!

Step 1. Grammar & Vocabulary Check:

Grammar Mastery

1. Necessity & Lack of Necessity

• have to / has to: Used for obligations or rules (external necessity).

• don't have to: Used when something is not necessary (it is optional).

  • • Example: "You have to lock the front door from inside."
    (Section 2 - Rule from host father)
  • • Example: "You don't have to borrow money from the bank."
    (Section 3 - Advice on budgeting)

2. Permission & Prohibitions

• can: Used for permission (you are allowed to do something).

• can't: Used for things that are forbidden or not allowed.

  • • Example: "You can come and go when you want."
    (Section 2 - House rule)
  • • Example: "No-one can sleep in your room apart from you."
    (Section 2 - Prohibition)

3. Future Predictions

• will / 'll: Used to predict future results or events.

• won't: Used for negative predictions (will not).

• Note: We often use I'm sure or I think with will.

  • • Example: "You will definitely miss their cooking."
    (Reading - Advice on independence)
  • • Example: "I'm sure you won't have much money left."
    (Reading - Warning on spending)
