Core Reading: Core Reading: Not Just All in the Mind

Education is a journey that looks different for everyone, whether they are switching schools across borders or navigating the digital world. Rebecca, for instance, experienced a major change when she moved from England to Spain. In her English primary school, she could enjoy group work and creative projects with the same teacher all day. However, in Spain, she had to change teachers for different subjects and face a much heavier workload. She remembers crying because she had to do hours of homework every night, whereas her friends back in England could finish their tasks much faster. This contrast shows how educational environments can vary significantly between countries.

This shift in how we study is also being shaped by technology. As explored in "A Web of Lies?", the Internet has made information more accessible, but it has also made cheating easier. In the past, if a student wanted to write an essay, they had to go to a library and physically copy words from books. Nowadays, plagiarism is a huge concern for teachers. If a student copies an entire essay from a website, they will not learn the actual material. Furthermore, if they rely only on the Web, they will lose the ability to think critically. However, technology also brings solutions; if teachers use sophisticated software, they will detect copied work immediately.

Beyond the classroom, the road to the future is filled with different choices. Ollie, a Year 9 student, is already contemplating his life after school. If he saves enough money, he will try to take a gap year to travel. His father, however, wants him to stay in the academic system and go straight to university to study Business. On the other hand, students like Pep are already encountering the reality of higher education. Studying Pure Mathematics is intellectually demanding, especially since he has to do his whole degree in English. Pep knows that if he gets the required grades, he will probably apply for a Master’s degree in Astrophysics to follow his passion.

Even after university, the learning process never truly ends. Dhanya studied International Law in France, but she found the courses very theoretical. She believes she has learned much more since she started working. As she says, "You can't beat the university of life." This reminds us that while we had to learn academic facts in school, practical experience is just as important. If you work hard and remain ambitious for success, you will find that every challenge is an opportunity to grow. The tutors and supportive peers we meet along the way also play a crucial role in our development.

In conclusion, whether we are dealing with the stress of exams, the temptation of the Internet, or the pressure of choosing a career, our education shapes who we become. If we embrace both modern technology and traditional values, we will be better prepared for the diverse world that waits for us after graduation. Education is not just about knowledge acquisition; it is about developing social skills and responsibility for our own future.

1-3. 📖 Reading: Breakfast around the world

A WEB OF LIES?

Jane Wilshere explores the effect that the World Wide Web is having on school life. The incredible growth of the Internet over recent years has caused problems for parents and teachers. Parents worry about which sites their children spend time on, while for teachers, the main worry is the way the Internet makes cheating easier!

Schools and universities say there has been a huge increase in plagiarism – taking other people's words and ideas and pretending that they are your own. In the past, anyone who wanted to copy had to go to a library, find the right books, read through them, find the sections they needed and then physically write down the words they wanted to use.

Nowadays, though, students can simply copy extracts from websites. Despite all this, we shouldn't assume that the Internet only brings problems. Indeed, you could say that for every problem the Internet creates, it also brings a solution. New software now helps teachers to detect copied work immediately.

"Some students suddenly start using words they can't possibly understand," explains one teacher. "In fact, one of my students recently gave me an essay he'd copied from the Web and it even contained Web advertisements – AND the name of the original author!"

As more and more schools install computers, the role of the teacher is changing. Making sure students don't just copy things and do learn how to quote work properly is part of their job. My 13-year-old son is currently working on a project about trees in our town. He has to photograph them and write about their characteristics.

This is all uploaded onto a website, and his group discusses their work online. If they finish the project, they will give a PowerPoint presentation about their ideas to the class. Instead of helping him cheat, the Web is actually helping him learn about school subjects, while also developing his life skills!

Section 3: Writing Practice

Step 1. Grammar & Vocabulary Check:

Grammar Mastery

1. Past Necessity and Ability (had to / could)

• had to: Used to express an obligation or necessity in the past (The past of 'must' or 'have to').

• could: Used to express a general ability or permission in the past (The past of 'can').

  • • Example: "Rebecca had to finish loads of homework every night." (Section 1 - Her experience in the Spanish school system)
  • • Example: "In England, students could enjoy more group work." (Section 1 - Comparison of classroom activities)

2. First Conditional (Real Possibilities)

• Structure: If + Subject + Present Simple, Subject + will + verb.

• Usage: To talk about a possible future situation and its result.

  • • Example: "If Ollie saves enough money, he will take a gap year." (Section 1 - Ollie's future travel plans)
  • • Example: "If students copy from the web, they will not develop critical thinking." (Section 1 - The risk of plagiarism)

3. Questions with "will" and "had to"

• Past Question: Did + Subject + have to + verb...?

• Future Question: What will + Subject + do if...?

  • • Example: "Did Rebecca have to change teachers every hour?" (Section 1 - Yes, she did in Spain)
  • • Example: "What will Pep do if he gets good grades?" (Section 1 - He will apply for a Master's degree)
