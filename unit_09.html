Core Reading: Not Just All in the Mind

Part 1: The Delicate Balance of Nature

In our modern world, we often receive news that reminds us how deeply we depend on the environment. Recently, an alarming article revealed that honeybees have been dying for unknown reasons. This is critical because if bees become extinct, the lack of pollination would mean we won't have any fruit or vegetables to eat. While some people simply enjoy a forecast of hot and sunny weather to plan a trip to the beach, others are looking at the bigger picture of conservation.

In Morovia, for instance, the government decided to pull down several horrible houses that had been built along the coast to create a new national park instead. These stories highlight a growing need for green spaces and research funding to protect the countryside. Nature is not just a backdrop for our lives; it is a complex system that requires active protection and scientific understanding.

Part 2: Man’s Best Friends and Unexpected Allies

Our relationship with the animal kingdom often produces extraordinary stories that defy common expectations. Animals are not just pets; they are participants in our social and functional lives. For example, a couple in Dorset got married with their dogs as part of the ceremony because the pets had brought the couple together when they first met on a beach.

Science also shows that how we treat animals affects their productivity. Researchers found that cows that had been given names produced 300 litres more milk than those which hadn't been named. Even animals with "bad reputations," like rats, are saving lives in Africa. Because they are small and have an incredible sense of smell, they can detect landmines without making them explode.

However, our interaction with nature is not always positive. A very rare megamouth shark recently died in a net after it had been caught by mistake. Despite efforts to persuade the fisherman to save the body for science, it was used for a traditional dish. From parrots taking orders in Japanese restaurants to a dog named Scooby appearing in court because he had been present at a crime scene, animals continue to play pivotal roles in the human world.

Part 3: The Science of Human Nature

While we study the nature of animals, science also investigates the darker side of human nature through psychology. One of the most famous studies was conducted by Stanley Milgram to understand why people participated in terrible crimes during the Second World War.

In the experiment, "teachers" were told to give electric shocks to "learners". Although the shocks were fake, the teachers believed they were real because the learners, who were actually actors, had complained of heart problems or had shouted in pain. When the teachers wanted to stop, a "scientist" in a white coat told them they must go on.

The results were shocking: 65% of participants gave the final 450-volt shock just because a person in authority told them to. This study showed that ordinary people find it extremely difficult to say "no" to authority figures, especially those in uniform. It revealed that the impulse to obey can sometimes override a person’s own conscience.

Scooby, who had been in the flat, was brought into court to see how he would react to the man suspected of the crime. It barked very loudly. The police now need to decide if there is enough evidence to take the case further.

Section 3: Writing Practice

Step 1. Grammar & Vocabulary Check:

Grammar Mastery

1. Form and Structure (had + past participle)

• Affirmative: Subject + had + past participle (e.g., worked, seen).

• Negative: Subject + hadn't (had not) + past participle.

  • • Example: "The pets had brought the couple together."
    (Section 1 - How Andrew and Harriet met)
  • • Example: "The cows hadn't been named by the farmers."
    (Section 1 - Comparison of milk production)

2. Expressing "The Past of the Past"

• Usage: We use the past perfect to talk about an action that happened before another action in the past.

• Sequence: Past Perfect (1st Action) → Past Simple (2nd Action).

  • • Example: "The shark died after it had been caught."
    (Section 1 - Caught in the net came before dying)
  • • Example: "The owner had been found dead in her flat."
    (Section 1 - The death happened before the investigation started)

3. Questions and Short Answers

• Question: Had + Subject + past participle...?

• Short Answer: Yes, I had. / No, I hadn't.

  • • Example: "Had the parrots lived in a cage before?"
    (Section 1 - Yes, they had lived there previously)
