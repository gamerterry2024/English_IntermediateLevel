Core Reading: Moments that Shape History

History is often thought of as a collection of significant dates and remarkable political shifts, but it is also composed of the personal stories and social gatherings that define our existence. On the streets of the UK, if you ask average citizens about Kazakhstan, they might only mention playing them at football. Yet, this vast nation, the 9th largest in the world, has a complex history dating back to the Stone Age. Getting to know the extraordinary transition of the Kazakh people helps us understand how traditional lifestyles evolved into modern economies.

For many centuries, the Silk Road trade route went through the region, leading to the founding of cities. The Kazakh people, meaning 'free spirits', were nomadic groups living off agriculture. This distinct culture survived until the 1800s, when the country was occupied by Russia. During the following decade, drastic changes resulted in hunger and tension, eventually leading to fighting in 1916. After joining the Soviet Union in 1920, the last nomads were forced to live on farms or to work in industry. It was only after gaining independence in 1991 that Kazakhstan’s economy began growing rapidly, transforming it into a global leader of oil production.

While nations undergo massive transformations, individuals also experience pivotal moments that they remember for as long as they live. These personal milestones often coincide with broader historical contexts. For instance, a man growing up in Mississippi in the 1960s might recall being treated as a second-class citizen due to segregation. For him, the day Barack Obama was elected to become President was a truly remarkable achievement that signified a shift in racial history. Similarly, someone might feel a real connection with the place of their ancestors after going on a trip to a small village in Ukraine, finding it very moving to spend a night in a house where their great-grandmother was born.

commemorating the past is a fundamental human trait. We see this in the way millions mourn the tragedy of Princess Diana’s death on the anniversary of August 31st. People regret not being able to go to London for her funeral, demonstrating how one influential figure can touch the lives of people as far away as Brazil. Even physical challenges can become historical markers in a person's life. On March 24th, a person might celebrate the anniversary of having a special operation that provided a second chance in life. After losing 75 kilos, they commit to going to the gym every day, proving that individual determination can overcome chronic health conditions like diabetes.

Finally, history is celebrated through social events. Whether it is attending a wedding in an old castle on the coast or going to a house-warming party in a converted church, these gatherings allow us to share experiences. A friend might spend ages getting everything ready for a dinner party for twelve, following recipes to cook aubergines for their guests. Even if an awkward encounter with an ex arriving with a new girlfriend ruins the night, these anecdotes become part of our own personal chronicles. From the peak of Mount Kinabalu at sunrise to the dance floor of a wedding full all evening, every moment contributes to the narrative of who we are. reflecting on these dates and events helps us appreciate the intricate tapestry of human history.

1-3. 📖 Reading: History of Kazakhstan

Ask most people on the streets of the UK what they know about Kazakhstan and the only thing they can say is 'We played them at football.' Ask where it is, and they may mention it's near Russia, but that's all. Yet Kazakhstan is huge - the 9th largest country in the world and the size of Western Europe. We think it's time people got to know it better. Oh, and yes, it is near Russia - they share a border of 6,846 kilometres!

People have lived in the region since the Stone Age. The society was nomadic - Kazakh comes from a word meaning 'free spirit' - with different groups living off seasonal agriculture and animals such as goats, sheep and horses, that fed on the steppe grassland. For many centuries, the Silk Road trade route went through the region, which led to the founding of cities such as Talaz, now 2,000 years old.

Islam was introduced by the Arabs in the 8th century, and Genghis Kahn's Mongol army invaded in 1219. Over the next 200 years, a distinct Kazakh language, culture and economy emerged, although still based on nomadic life.

This traditional lifestyle changed during the 1800s, when the country was occupied by Russia. The political and economic changes and a population growth caused by people settling in the region resulted in hunger and tension. It eventually led to fighting in 1916, followed by a civil war.

In 1920, Kazakhstan became part of the communist Soviet Union. Over the following decade, the last Kazakh nomads were forced to live on farms or work in industry. Other people within the Soviet Union, including Germans, Ukrainians and Koreans, were also sent to work there.

After gaining independence in 1991, Kazakhstan’s economy grew rapidly. It's now the 11th largest producer of oil and gas as well as exporting many other natural resources.

Step 1. Grammar & Vocabulary Check:

Grammar Mastery

1. Prepositions and Nouns (Time & Place)

• in / on / since / during: Used to specify when an event happened in history or a person's life.

• in / at / on / through: Used to describe where a location is or the movement through a region.

  • • Example: "People have lived in the region since the Stone Age." (Section 0 - Kazakhstan's early history)
  • • Example: "Princess Diana died on August 31st." (Section 0 - Personal milestones)

2. -ing Forms (Gerunds & Participles)

• After / Before + -ing: Used to describe the order of events (sequence).

• Noun + -ing: Used to provide more information about a person, group, or thing.

  • • Example: "After gaining independence in 1991, the economy grew." (Section 0 - Recent Kazakhstan history)
  • • Example: "Kazakh comes from a word meaning 'free spirit'." (Section 0 - Etymology of Kazakh)

3. Verb Patterns (to-infinitive / -ing)

• Verb + to + Verb: Used with verbs like force, decide, hope to show intent or requirement.

• Verb + -ing: Used with verbs like spend (time), regret, remember to describe past actions or duration.

  • • Example: "Nomads were forced to live on farms." (Section 0 - Soviet Union era)
  • • Example: "I spent ages getting everything ready." (Section 2 - Social gatherings)
