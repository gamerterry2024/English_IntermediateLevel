Core Reading: The Diverse Ways We Relax and Play

The concept of relaxation varies significantly across different cultures, yet the fundamental need to "take it easy" remains a universal human trait. Whether it is through physical activity, spectator sports, or simply catching up on sleep, how we choose to spend our free time defines our lifestyle and well-being. This unit explores the fascinating balance between active leisure and essential rest.

For many, the weekend is the primary time to recharge. Some people prefer high-energy outdoor activities, such as hiking in the mountains or camping in the forest with family. These plans often depend on the weather forecast, as a sunny and warm weekend provides the perfect opportunity for a walk in nature or a refreshing swim in a clear river. Others, however, choose a more sedentary approach, opting to stay at home, read a book, or watch a movie to recover from a long work week.

When it comes to entertainment, sports play a massive role in global culture. Football, often cited as the world’s most popular sport, attracts billions of viewers. Its simplicity—requiring no expensive gear or complex skills to start—makes it accessible to everyone. Even when a big game ends in a boring nil-nil draw, the unpredictability of the next match keeps fans coming back. However, beyond mainstream football, many countries cherish their own unique national traditions. In Argentina, 'Pato' combines history and horsemanship, while in Japan, 'Keirin' turns cycling into a high-speed, high-stakes spectator event. Newer inventions like 'Bossaball' even blend music and gymnastics, showing that the way we play is constantly evolving.

While active sports are exciting, the importance of rest cannot be overlooked. In Spain, the tradition of the 'siesta'—a short nap after lunch—has long been a cultural staple. Although modern work schedules have made it a luxury for some, recent research highlights its immense benefits. A short nap can increase energy levels, improve mood, and make workers more productive. In fact, companies in cities like New York and London are now installing high-tech napping pods, recognizing that tired employees lose concentration and make more mistakes.

Ultimately, whether we are dancing at a birthday party, betting on a fast-paced cycle race, or enjoying a quiet nap, the goal is the same: to find a healthy balance. Relaxing isn't just about being lazy; it's about staying healthy, reducing stress, and making the most of our time so we can stay happy and productive in our daily lives.

1-3. 📖 Reading: Unique Sports Around the World

Pato: The National Sport of Argentina

Although it doesn't attract big crowds or money, Pato is the national sport of Argentina. It was invented in the 17th century and originally involved two teams on horses trying to prevent each other from carrying a duck (pato in Spanish) to their farmhouse. The sport was banned for a while because of violence - not only to the duck, but also to other players. Some were killed in fights or from being kicked by the horses. The modern game (sometimes also known as Horseball) is a lot safer. Teams of four riders fight for the 'duck' (now a ball with handles) and throw and catch it to try and score in their opponent's net.

Keirin: Japan's High-Speed Cycle Racing

Since 1948, Keirin has become one of Japan's biggest spectator sports with over 20 million a year attending events. People now bet over $15 billion dollars on the outcome of the races. Keirin is like horseracing, but with cyclists. Nine competitors ride round a track following a cyclist who sets the pace at about 50km/h. He then leaves the track so the riders can race each other for the remaining two laps, reaching speeds of 70km/h. There are often crashes as there is so little space to race in. Riders have to train 15 hours a day in special schools to be able to race and can win millions of dollars.

Bossaball: A Fusion of Music and Sport

Bossaball is a new game with a small but growing support. It was invented in Belgium a few years ago. It's played on a special inflatable pitch and is a mixture of volleyball, football, gymnastics and dance. Players bounce up and down and aim to pass, kick and head the ball over the net. The rules are a little complicated, but you basically lose a point when the ball touches the floor. The referee is also a DJ who plays Brazilian music as the teams play.

Step 1. Grammar & Vocabulary Check:

Grammar Mastery

1. Talking About the Future

In English, we choose different forms depending on how sure we are about a plan.

  • • Might + Verb: Used for possibility. You are not 100% sure.
    Example (Section 2): "The weather forecast says it might rain."
  • • Present Continuous (am/is/are + -ing): Used for fixed arrangements. The plan is already made.
    Example (Section 2): "I’m watching Bossaball this weekend."
  • • Be going to + Verb: Used for intentions or plans decided before speaking.
    Example (Section 0): "Some people are going to stay at home to recover."

2. Superlatives (The Best of the Best)

We use superlatives to compare three or more things and identify the top one.

• Short Adjectives: the + adjective + -est (e.g., big → the biggest)

• Long Adjectives: the most + adjective (e.g., popular → the most popular)

• Irregular: good → the best / bad → the worst
