Core Reading: The Power of Culture: From Small Screens to Global Scenes

Culture is not just about visiting a museum; it is a living part of our daily lives that shapes who we are and how we connect with others. Across the globe, people have been discovering new ways to express their passions through music, film, and art. Whether it is a low-budget movie from Nigeria or a classical concert in Venezuela, the world of culture is amazing and full of surprising stories.

One of the most fascinating success stories in modern culture is the rise of "Nollywood." It might be surprising to learn that the third biggest film industry in the world—behind the United States and India—is based in Nigeria. For decades, Nigerian filmmakers have been producing over 500 films a year. Unlike Hollywood movies, which take a year to film, these movies take an average of just ten days to complete! Because they are made on tiny budgets, many viewers are amazed that they can generate so much money. Nollywood is the home of voodoo horror movies, and although the special effects aren't like Harry Potter, the stories are never boring. In fact, fans have been buying these DVDs at street stalls for years because they offer a way to escape from the tiring problems of everyday life.

Similarly, music has the power to change lives. Take Gustavo, for example. He has been playing the trumpet for ten years with El Sistema, a program that helps young people from poor backgrounds. Without this opportunity, he might have been in a depressing situation, but instead, he has been rehearsing difficult Russian masterpieces like The Rite of Spring. He finds the music fantastic and believes it has given him a new future.

On the other hand, some people find their passion in modern pop culture. Peter, a dedicated music fan, has been learning Turkish for two years because he is interested in the singer Sertab Erener. After hearing her win the Eurovision Song Contest, he became obsessed with her voice. He has even traveled to Istanbul to see her sing live! For Peter, culture is not just something to watch; it is a reason to learn a new language and explore a wonderful new country.

However, culture also brings debate. When two friends discussed going to the movies after a boring lecture, they found that their tastes were very different. One friend had already seen the film Dust and Heat, but found it disappointing because the ending was too predictable. While one was excited about a horror movie, the other felt frightened by the genre and preferred something lighter. This shows that while some art is brilliant to one person, it can be annoying to another.

Even literature and fine art are changing. Zelda has been watching a TV series based on the detective Wallander, but she feels annoyed because the main character is different from her imagination. Meanwhile, Mary, an art student, has been studying sculpture and recently saw an extraordinary exhibition by Giacometti. She was fascinated by his thin, strange sculptures.

In conclusion, whether we have been reading crime thrillers or have been making low-budget films, culture remains essential. It provides incredible wealth to some and satisfied feelings to others. As we continue exploring, we realize that the world of culture is far more diverse and exciting than we ever imagined.

1-3. 📖 Reading: Welcome to Nollywood!

Femi Abulu introduces the weird and wonderful world of Nigerian cinema.

It might surprise you to learn that the third biggest film industry in the world – behind the United States and India – is based in Nigeria. Producing over 500 films a year, the industry generates around £160 million a year. The majority of films are made on budgets of between £10,000 and £20,000, and take an average of just ten days to complete. Compare this to the £65 million and year-long filming period needed to make the average Hollywood movie! However, while they may be cheap to produce, Nigerian films obviously still make good money.

They are not designed for the cinema. Instead, they are filmed on video cameras and copied directly onto video cassettes or DVDs, which are then sold at street stalls and tiny shops not just in Nigeria, but right across Africa. Most films are recorded in English, as it represents a common, neutral language in a country of 150 million people, where over 230 different languages are spoken. While Hollywood specialises in action movies and the Indian Bollywood studios produce amazing musicals, Nollywood – as the Nigerian film industry is known – is the home of voodoo horror movies: films about witchcraft and black magic.

It all began in 1992, with Living in Bondage, a terrible tale about a man who becomes involved with a religious cult that promises incredible wealth if he kills his wife. Since then, these kinds of movies have become increasingly popular, with Nigeria’s most famous director, Chico Ejiro, being at the centre of the boom. Ejiro has directed over 80 movies, and despite the poor sound quality and predictable endings, his films are much loved.

Not all Nollywood films are horror movies, though. Gangster movies are also popular, as are success stories. Some dramas deal with current issues such as AIDS, corruption and women’s rights. However, certain subjects are generally avoided and films are occasionally banned because they may cause tensions in a country with people from so many different backgrounds and which has experienced civil war.

In the end, the films about witches are popular for much the same reasons as Harry Potter or Lord of the Rings, even if they don't have quite the same special effects or amazing costumes. They are fantasies where the bad people are punished and the good people rewarded: the cheating husband loses his physical powers, or an evil love rival goes crazy, or the poor man becomes rich. Films are generally about escaping from the problems of everyday life and in a tough society like Nigeria this becomes even more important!

Step 1. Grammar & Vocabulary Check:

Grammar Mastery

1. Present Perfect Continuous (Duration)

• have/has + been + -ing: Used to talk about an action that started in the past and is still continuing now.

• for / since: Used with this tense to show how long the action has been happening.

  • • Example: "I have been learning Turkish for the last two years." (Speaker 1 - Peter's language study)
  • • Example: "We have been rehearsing The Rite of Spring recently." (Speaker 2 - Gustavo's orchestra practice)

2. Adjectives with -ing (Describing Things)

• -ing adjectives: Used to describe the characteristic of a person, thing, or situation.

• common words: amazing, fascinating, boring, tiring, surprising, frightening.

  • • Example: "The stories are extra interesting." (Speaker 3 - Zelda's opinion on books)
  • • Example: "He does these beautiful, strange, thin sculptures." (Speaker 4 - Mary's description of art)

3. Adjectives with -ed (Describing Feelings)

• -ed adjectives: Used to describe how a person feels about something.

• common words: interested, bored, excited, frightened, annoyed, worried.

  • • Example: "I’ve become much more interested in sculpture." (Speaker 4 - Mary's change in taste)
  • • Example: "His films are much loved." (Reading - Fans' feelings toward Chico Ejiro)
